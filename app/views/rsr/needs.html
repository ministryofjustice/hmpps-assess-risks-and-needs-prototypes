Suitability refers to the appropriateness and adequacy of the accommodation and the relationship the individual has with the other adults in it. Take into account the person’s capacity to live independently, bearing in mind the challenges often faced by individuals with a hidden disability.

This question is concerned with the individual’s pattern of drinking, rather than the frequency or level of consumption.

Binge drinking is defined as drinking too much alcohol over a short period, for example, over the course of an evening. It typically leads to drunkenness. It has immediate and short-term risks to the drinker and to those around them.

A binge is defined as consumption of more than twice the Government’s recommended daily limit in a single session (8 units for men and 6 units for women). However, many binge drinkers consume substantially more than this, or drink this amount more rapidly.

Binge drinking tends to involve periods of sporadic excessive consumption of alcohol interspersed with periods of relative abstinence. The individual may consume very little or no alcohol for days, weeks or even months, but will then drink large quantities, becoming quickly intoxicated.

A binge may last for a number of hours or for a period of days. A common pattern is to abstain during the working week, but drink excessively at weekends. This pattern of consumption, involving periods of near abstinence or moderate drinking interspersed with binges, can be extremely problematic in terms of health and risk of reconviction.

Consider:

  • the number of episodes of excessive alcohol consumption in the last 6 months
  • how long the binges last
  • whether the binges are a weekly occurrence - for example, when the individual gets paid - or if are they less frequent
  • whether there is any pattern to the binges or if there is an identifiable trigger, for example, after visiting their children

No problems

The individual:

  • elicits no evidence to suggest that they are a binge drinker
  • has not started drinking excessively on occasions in the last 6 months
  • does not drink alcohol at all or only drinks alcohol in moderation
  • has previously had an alcohol misuse problem with excessive alcohol consumption or binge drinking, but has not consumed alcohol for over 1 year
  • drinks on a regular basis, but does not have a pattern of binge drinking, even if their overall alcohol intake is at a level which may lead to problems

Some problems

The individual:

  • has a pattern of drinking, which could be described as binges, for example, drinking heavily at weekends, but this has not resulted in the serious problems described above
  • does not see anything wrong in their behaviour - dismissing it as just ‘having a good night out’ or ‘letting their hair down’
  • may spend beyond their means on binges and the binges may coincide with periods of offending
  • has shown less frequent incidents of excessive drinking, if this appears to be an increasing phenomena and possibly linked to offending

Significant problems

The individual:

  • admits to, or there is evidence that they binge drink
  • binge drinking has a detrimental effect on all areas of their life
  • may have experienced drinking to unconsciousness, blackouts and being unable to account for periods of time when drinking
  • may have missed work or experienced relationship difficulties due to this behaviour
  • may be aware of the detrimental effects of their behaviour but be unable to stop, or they may not recognise the problem
  • may spend all their available money on alcohol when binge drinking
  • has binge drinking episodes that will often be directly related to incidents of offending
  • has had several incidents of excessive alcohol use in the last six months, which do not amount to a binge pattern, but are ‘out of character,’ especially if these incidents were clearly related to their offending
